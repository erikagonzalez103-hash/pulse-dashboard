Consumer Validation · Man-on-the-Street Interviews

Companion to the Pulse Dallas Market Research Brief

Interviewer Instructions

Purpose

This guide structures 10–20 minute conversations with potential Pulse users. Each interview is a chance to confirm or disprove a specific hypothesis from the Market Research Brief. The questions are designed to surface authentic discovery behavior, not to sell Pulse.

Interviewees should leave the conversation feeling listened to, not pitched. The goal is signal, not buy-in.

Logistics

Length: 10–20 minutes per conversation.

Locations: Klyde Warren Park (mixed demographics, midday/evening), Bishop Arts Saturday foot traffic, Katy Trail (active users, dog owners), NorthPark Center (parents, broader demos), Deep Ellum on a Friday or Saturday evening (younger crowds).

Target: 10–15 conversations across the four primary segments. Aim for a minimum of 2 per segment.

Recording: Ask permission. If granted, record audio for sizzle-reel quotes. If declined, take written notes.

Compensation: Offer a $10 gift card or coffee in exchange for participation. Most people will say no — always offer.

How to use this document

Each section maps to a specific validation trigger in §9 of the Market Research Brief. Sidebar notes flag what to listen for. Branch logic indicates which questions to ask based on screening responses — do not ask all questions of all interviewees.

If a section doesn’t apply (e.g., Section 4.2 if the interviewee has no children), skip it. Total core questions: 12. Total possible questions including branched probes: 22.

Things to listen for (general)

Frustration that surfaces unprompted is more valuable than frustration that requires probing.

Specific tools named (“I use Beli for restaurants, IG for nightlife, Yelp for dog-friendly”) reveal current discovery stack.

Quotes about emotion, not just process — “I hate when…”, “I wish there was…”, “I just give up and order in.” These are sizzle-reel material.

Disconfirmations are findings. If 5 of 10 interviewees say they’re happy with Google Maps and don’t feel a gap, that is a meaningful result.

Section 1. Warm-Up & Screening

Time: 2–3 min

Establish rapport, gather basic demographics, and identify which persona track to follow.

Q1.1 — “Tell me a little about yourself — do you live in Dallas?”

Listen for neighborhood, length of residence, and whether they’re a visitor. Visitors get the Section 5 visitor branch.

Q1.2 — “Roughly how often do you go out — restaurants, bars, coffee, beauty appointments, events, that kind of thing?”

Looking for frequency. “Multiple times a week” and “once a week” are core users. “Once a month or less” suggests a low-frequency segment that’s harder to monetize.

Q1.3 — “Do you live with anyone? Kids, partner, dog, roommates?”

This identifies which segment branches apply. Note their answer — you’ll want it later.

Section 2. Discovery Behavior Today

Time: 3–5 min · Maps to §9.3 (Behavior triggers)

Q2.1 — “When you want to try somewhere new, walk me through what you actually do. Not what you should do — what do you actually do?”

This is the most important question in the guide. Don’t accept “I just Google it” — probe for the actual sequence. (“What do you Google? Then what?”)

Listen for: Google Maps, Yelp, Instagram, TikTok, friends, group chat, Beli, The Infatuation, Reddit, Eater, Resy.

Q2.2 — “How many actually-new places have you tried in the last month?”

Numeric answer. <2/month is low frequency, 3–5 is core, 6+ is power user.

Q2.3 — “What’s a recent time you couldn’t find what you were looking for? Or you tried somewhere and it sucked?”

Pain points emerge here. Quotes from this question are gold.

Section 3. The Real-Time Premise

Time: 2–3 min · Maps to §9.3 (Q11/Q12 triggers)

Q3.1 — “How much does ‘right now’ matter when you’re picking somewhere? Like, is it busy now, is there a wait, is there a happy hour going on?”

Validates the real-time premise. If they shrug, the real-time positioning is weaker than assumed. If they light up, it’s confirmed.

Q3.2 — “If there was a way to see what was popping in Dallas right now — places filling up, places running offers tonight, things you’d miss otherwise — would you actually use that?”

This is the closest to a Pulse pitch. Don’t lead with brand. Listen to the qualifier in their answer — “yeah, if it actually worked” vs “maybe” vs “probably not.”

Section 4. Persona-Specific Probes

Time: 3–7 min · Maps to §9.1 (Persona triggers). Branch by interviewee profile from Q1.3.

4.1 If young professional, no kids, no dog (or dog left at home)

Q4.1.a — “When you’re going out with friends, who picks the place? How does that decision actually happen?”

Listen for group-chat dynamics, who gets veto power, and what makes a place “safe” to suggest.

Q4.1.b — “Have you ever shown up somewhere and immediately wished you’d gone somewhere else? What happened?”

Crowd-fit, vibe, busy/empty mismatch. These are all real-time problems Pulse can solve.

4.2 If parent (kids under 18 in household)

Hypothesis: Elevated parents are underserved. Validation question: do they actually feel the pain, or have private networks already solved it?

Q4.2.a — “When you go out with the kids, what kills a place for you? What do you wish was easier to figure out before you got there?”

Q4.2.b — “Do you ever feel like you’re downgrading to be ‘kid-friendly’? What does that mean to you?”

This is the Migoni quote validation. If parents agree they’re downgrading, the elevated-parent segment is real.

Q4.2.c — “How do you currently find spots that work — mom groups, friends, Instagram, something else?”

If they have a strong private network solution, that’s a disconfirmation signal.

4.3 If dog owner (and takes dog out at least sometimes)

Q4.3.a — “How often do you take your dog with you somewhere — a patio, a coffee shop, an event?”

Q4.3.b — “How do you figure out where you can take them?”

BringFido, Google Maps photos (looking for dog photos in reviews), word of mouth, Dallasites101, etc.

Q4.3.c — “Has there been a time you skipped going somewhere because you couldn’t bring the dog and didn’t feel like leaving them?”

If yes, dog-compatibility kills demand — a strong validation signal.

4.4 If wellness-engaged (mentioned spa, gym, beauty in Q1.2)

Q4.4.a — “When you book a new beauty or wellness spot — a new salon, a new yoga studio, a new spa — how do you decide?”

Q4.4.b — “How often do you actually try somewhere new vs sticking with your regulars?”

If they’re loyal-only and rarely try new, the wellness-discovery use case is weaker. If they actively explore, it’s confirmed.

4.5 If visitor (lives outside Dallas)

Q4.5.a — “What brought you to Dallas? How long are you here?”

Q4.5.b — “How are you figuring out where to eat / drink / spend your free time while you’re here?”

Q4.5.c — “Would you have downloaded an app for this trip? What would have made you?”

Visitor download intent is hard to fake — they’ll either feel the friction or not.

Section 5. Filter Testing

Time: 3–4 min · Maps to §9.2 (Filter triggers)

Read the filter list aloud. Ask the interviewee to identify which would actually change their behavior. The bathroom filter is intentionally listed alongside the others to avoid biasing the response.

Q5.1 — “If I told you a discovery app could filter for any of these, which would actually change where you went?”

Read this list slowly:

Vibe / aesthetic

Crowd fit (right age, right energy)

Trending right now

Walkable / parking available

Dog-friendly

Kid-friendly

Clean restrooms

Reservation availability tonight

Quiet enough to talk

Open late

Live offer or discount

Accessibility (mobility, sensory)

Listen for which filter the interviewee elaborates on unprompted. The bathroom filter, in particular, often gets a strong reaction one way or the other — capture exact quotes.

Q5.2 — “Is there a filter I didn’t mention that would matter to you?”

Open-ended discovery. Anything mentioned by 3+ interviewees becomes a v1 feature candidate.

Section 6. Concept Reaction (Soft Pitch)

Time: 2–3 min · Maps to §9.3 (Q15)

Now — and only now — describe Pulse briefly. Use this exact framing or close to it:

“There’s an app being built called Pulse, just for Dallas. It shows what’s actually trending right now — across food, drink, beauty, wellness, things to do — personalized to your preferences. Vendors can post live offers. It’s free for users.” — Standardized concept pitch

Q6.1 — “On a scale of 1–5, how likely are you to try something like that?”

≥4 across most segments validates the concept. ≤3 average disconfirms.

Q6.2 — “What would make you actually keep using it after the first week?”

This is the retention question. Listen for what they’d need to see to come back.

Q6.3 — “Who do you know who’d love it? Who’d hate it?”

Network effect read. If they instantly name 3 friends, you have viral seed. If they shrug, the concept doesn’t feel shareable yet.

Section 7. Wrap-Up & Sizzle

Time: 1–2 min

Q7.1 — “If this app existed and worked exactly the way you’re imagining, what would you tell a friend about it?”

This is the on-camera-quote question. The answer becomes investor deck material if it’s good. Always ask permission to attribute (first name + neighborhood is plenty).

Q7.2 — “Last thing — anything I didn’t ask that I should have?”

The best insights often come in the final two minutes when the interviewee feels relaxed.

Thank them. Hand them a card or a way to follow Pulse if they want updates. Note their first name, neighborhood, segment, and any standout quotes immediately after the conversation ends — memory degrades fast.

Validation Triggers Reference

Cross-reference each interview against these triggers from §9 of the Market Research Brief. After 10–15 interviews, score each segment confirmed / disconfirmed / inconclusive.

Each interview should produce 3–5 specific quotes captured verbatim, segment classification, and trigger-by-trigger notes. Synthesize after every 5 interviews — don’t wait for all 15 before reviewing.

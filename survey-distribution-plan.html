Survey Distribution Plan

1. Strategic Frame

This document is the operational plan for distributing the Pulse Dallas consumer survey. The goal is 100–300 qualified survey completions inside a 14-day window for under $200 in paid spend, with the majority of completions actually sourced from free organic channels.

The most important strategic point:

“The objective is qualified completions, not impressions, clicks, or downloads. Every campaign decision below — objective, audience, creative, channel mix — is calibrated to that one outcome.” — The single point of focus

Why a small Meta budget works

A common mistake is to assume $150–200 is too small to be useful. For survey distribution it’s adequate, for three reasons:

Survey ads have higher CTR than commerce ads (no purchase commitment, low-friction CTA), so impression efficiency is naturally better.

Meta’s Traffic objective optimizes against clicks within hours, not days — you don’t need conversion volume to teach the algorithm.

Paid Meta is one of three channels in this plan, not the whole plan. The other 70% of completions come free from organic channels (§6).

2. Campaign Architecture

Objective

Traffic. Not Conversions. With this budget, Conversions doesn’t have enough events to optimize from and will deliver inefficiently. Traffic optimizes for clicks against the survey URL.

Placements

Instagram Feed

Instagram Stories

Instagram Reels

Facebook Feed (if budget allows)

Skip Audience Network — click quality is poor for surveys.

Schedule

Total flight: 14 days

Days 1–3: All variants live, equal budget split

Days 4–5: Cull bottom 50% of variants by CTR; reallocate budget

Days 6–10: Top performers run at full budget

Days 11–14: Refresh creative or pause if completions plateau

Bid strategy

Use “Highest volume” (default) for the first 5 days.

Switch to manual bid cap at $0.80–$1.00 per click only if costs run high in days 6–7.

Don’t use Cost cap or Bid cap initially — they constrain learning.

3. Audience Strategy

Three ad sets, tested simultaneously. Treat the broad ad set as the control — it should outperform expectations more often than not.

Ad Set A — Broad Dallas (control)

Geo: Dallas–Fort Worth–Arlington DMA, plus 25-mile radius from city center

Age: 25–45

Gender: All

Interests: None (broad)

Languages: English, Spanish

Why broad works: Meta’s algorithm is now better at identifying intent from behavior than from interest tags. Broad ad sets typically outperform narrow ones for awareness-stage objectives.

Ad Set B — Lifestyle Layered

Geo: Same as A

Age: 25–45

Interests: any of — Restaurants, Dining out, Cocktail bars, Yoga, Pilates, Boutique fitness, Weekend brunch, Dog adoption, Pet care, Family activities, Travel, Concerts, Live music

This ad set serves as the persona-targeted variant. Useful for confirming whether interest-based targeting beats broad in this specific case.

Ad Set C — Lookalike (only if seed list ≥ 100)

1% lookalike of any existing email list (Pulse waitlist, vendor interview contacts, personal network)

Geo: same as A; Age: 25–45

Skip Ad Set C entirely if the seed list is under 100. Lookalikes built from small seeds are noise.

Exclusions

Exclude users who have already clicked the survey URL (Custom Audience from URL traffic). This prevents re-serving people who started but didn’t finish.

Exclude employees of competing apps (Beli, Yelp, Resy, Eater — small Custom Audiences via job title) if available. Optional.

4. Creative — Four Variants

Creative variants test angles, not word choice. Each one imagines a different person stopping their scroll for a different reason. Keep all four roughly equivalent in production quality so comparisons are fair.

Variant A — The Insider

Angle: Belonging. “You’re a real Dallas person; help us build the right thing.”

Headline: Help us build the Dallas discovery app you actually wish existed.

Body: 5-minute survey. $25 gift card raffle. We’re building Pulse — a real-time city discovery app launching in Dallas — and we’d rather get it right than guess.

CTA button: Take Survey

Visual direction: Dallas skyline at golden hour. Pulse wordmark in gold overlaid in upper right. Editorial, not stock-photo. Pexels CDN for sourcing per Pulse imagery convention.

Variant B — The Pain Point

Angle: Frustration. The shared experience of the ‘where should we go’ group chat loop.

Headline: Tired of the group chat “where should we go” loop?

Body: Same. So we’re building Pulse — a Dallas discovery app that actually answers the question. Take 5 minutes, help us shape it, win a $25 gift card.

CTA button: Help Build It

Visual direction: Phone screen mockup of a generic group chat with too many “idk where do you wanna go” messages. Slightly humorous. Navy + gold accent.

Variant C — Persona-Targeted (Dog)

Angle: Specific identity. Reader recognizes themselves.

Headline: If you’ve ever wondered ‘can my dog come?’ — read this.

Body: We’re building Pulse, a Dallas discovery app that filters for what actually matters to you — dog-friendly patios included. 5 minutes, $25 gift card raffle, we’d love your input.

CTA button: Take Survey

Visual direction: Dog on a Dallas patio at golden hour. Authentic, not staged. Owner partly visible in soft focus.

Note: Use this variant only against Ad Set B with dog-related interests, or as a standalone test. Don’t serve it broadly to non-owners.

Variant D — Reward-Forward

Angle: Direct value exchange. No story — just incentive.

Headline: 5 minutes. $25 gift card raffle. Help shape Dallas’s next discovery app.

Body: We’re Pulse. We’re launching in Dallas. Take 5 minutes, tell us how you actually find places, win a $25 gift card. That’s the deal.

CTA button: Take Survey

Visual direction: Clean type-driven design. The $25 figure prominent. Pulse navy + gold palette.

Production note: Static images are sufficient. Don’t over-invest in video for survey ads — production cost-to-yield ratio is poor at this budget level.

5. Budget Allocation

Reserve up to $50 for boosting the best-performing variant in week 2 if early signal is strong. If early signal is weak, pause spend and reinvest the remaining budget in additional raffle prizes (3 × $25) to lift completion on organic channels instead.

6. The 70% — Free Distribution Channels

Paid Meta should account for roughly 30% of total survey completions. The remaining 70% comes from free organic channels, which typically yield higher-quality, more articulate responses.

6.1 Reddit r/Dallas and r/askDallas

Pre-work: Build at least 50 karma in r/Dallas through genuine comments and contributions over 1–2 weeks before posting. Reddit moderators and users can identify and downvote naked promo.

Post format: Honest first-person framing. “Hey r/Dallas — I’m building something for Dallas and I’d love your input. Here’s the survey [link]. Happy to share results back when it closes.”

Time of day: Tuesday–Thursday, 9–11am Central. Avoid weekends and holidays.

Expected yield: 30–80 completions if the post lands well.

6.2 Facebook Groups

Always ask the admin for permission before posting. Most will say yes if you offer to share results back.

Dallas Foodies

Dallas Moms (and adjacent neighborhood mom groups)

Dallas Dog Parents

Dallas Young Professionals

Dallas Black Foodies

Dallas Latino Foodies

Neighborhood-specific groups (Bishop Arts residents, Knox-Henderson, etc.)

Yield per group: 5–20 completions if posted thoughtfully. Total potential: 30–120 completions across groups.

6.3 Nextdoor

Different demographic than Instagram — older, parents, homeowners. Useful for filling out the elevated-parents segment.

Post in a few specific neighborhood feeds rather than broad.

Yield: 10–30 completions; lower volume but high signal quality.

6.4 Personal Network

LinkedIn post: framed as a founder asking for help, not as marketing.

Personal Instagram: story + grid post. Ask for shares specifically, not just completions.

Email blast to your contacts: short, personal, 50–100 sends.

Yield: 20–60 completions, very high quality (people who know you take care).

6.5 Vendor Interview Closing

At the end of every vendor interview, ask: “Would you mind sharing this consumer survey with your customers? It helps the same effort.” Frame it as helping each other.

Vendor distribution channels: their Instagram, their email list, in-store flyers.

Yield per vendor: 5–25 completions if they actually share. Across 14 vendors, potentially 30–100 completions.

Strategic value: This is the strongest signal you can put in front of an investor — “we didn’t just talk to vendors and customers separately. Vendors helped us find customers. The supply side is already activated.”

7. Tracking & Attribution

Without tracking, you cannot tell which variant or which channel produced the most useful responses. UTM parameters are non-negotiable.

UTM scheme

Append a tagged version of your survey URL to each variant and each channel. Example structure:

https://yourform.com/pulse-survey?utm_source=meta&utm_medium=cpc&utm_campaign=pulse_survey&utm_content=variant_a

Capturing UTMs

In Google Forms: use the URL Parameter feature in form settings; map each UTM to a hidden form field.

In Typeform: use Hidden Fields feature; same mapping logic.

Verify before launch by visiting one tagged URL and checking that the hidden fields populate correctly.

What to compare across channels

Completion count (volume — obvious)

Completion rate (% of clicks that finish the survey — quality signal)

Time-on-survey (longer time often = more thoughtful answers)

Open-ended response length (the qualitative gold)

Demographic skew (to ensure no segment is over- or under-represented)

The highest-CTR variant is rarely the highest-quality variant. Use the channel-by-channel breakdown to weight findings appropriately.

8. Optimization Rules

Pre-decide what triggers a change so that decisions in-flight are mechanical, not emotional.

Daily checks (5 minutes)

Spend pacing on track? (Daily target = total budget ÷ 14)

Any ad set spending more than 2x its share? Pause if so.

Any creative variant with CTR below 0.5% after 1,000 impressions? Pause.

End of Day 3 review (30 minutes)

Rank variants by CTR. Pause bottom 50%.

Reallocate paused-variant budget to top performers.

If completion rate (clicks → finishes) is below 25%, the survey itself is too long or unclear — fix the survey, not the ads.

End of Day 7 review (60 minutes)

Total completions on track for goal? (Target: 50% of goal by day 7)

If yes: hold steady, refresh creative on day 10.

If no: deploy reserve budget against best variant; double organic posting cadence.

Kill criteria

Pause any variant under 0.5% CTR after 2,000 impressions.

Pause entire campaign if cost-per-completion exceeds $4 by day 7 — the survey or audience is the problem, not the spend.

Stop early if you hit 300 completions before day 14. Diminishing returns past that point.

9. Common Pitfalls

Surveying for too long. 5 minutes is the practical ceiling. The Pulse 20-question survey is right at the edge — kill any optional question that drags the timer past 5 minutes for the median respondent.

Treating Meta as the primary channel. It’s the smallest of the three by yield. Spend more energy on the organic channels.

Optimizing for completes when you should be optimizing for quality. A 100-completion thoughtful survey beats a 500-completion noisy one.

Targeting too narrowly out of the gate. Stacked interest layers raise CPMs and constrain delivery. Trust the broad audience to find the right people.

Running creative more than 7 days. Even good creative fatigues. Refresh the visual each week even if copy stays the same.

Forgetting to capture UTMs. Without them, the post-survey analysis is guesswork.

Pitching too hard in organic posts. Reddit and Facebook groups punish naked promotion. Lead with the ask, not the brand.

10. Definition of Done

The campaign is complete when the following are true:

≥150 total survey completions across paid and organic channels.

Representation across all four primary segments (§5 of the Brief), with at least 20 responses per segment.

UTM data attached to every response, allowing channel-by-channel analysis.

At least 30 open-ended responses to Q5 (skip reasons) and Q10 (category gaps).

Zero open spend on under-performing variants.

Synthesize findings against the validation triggers in §9 of the Market Research Brief and the Field Interview Guide. Every confirmed/disconfirmed hypothesis goes into the investor narrative.
